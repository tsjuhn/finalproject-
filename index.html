Revenue and Success in the world of FCS football

North Dakota State running back Chamar Brown runs with the football against Illinois State. Credit: Tim Sanger/NDSU Athletics

While the annual revenue of FCS football schools proves that those schools are the most successfull, it is also backed up advanced analytics.

By Troy Juhn

As of 2024, there are 129 teams competing in the Football Championship Subdivision (FCS), distributed across 13 conferences. In analyzing the financial investment in football programs at this level, data was obtained for 78 teams, spanning the years 2021 to 2023. This dataset includes schools that have since transitioned to the Football Bowl Subdivision (FBS), such as James Madison, Jacksonville State, and Kennesaw State.

Upon sorting the data by football revenue, 20 schools were identified as generating over $15 million in total revenue. Notably, James Madison led all programs in football investment, reporting expenditures exceeding $30 million, underscoring its substantial commitment to the sport during this period.

But since JMU, Kennesaw State and Jacksonville State have all debuted at the FBS level in the last few years, those schools weren’t factored into this visual with the top 17 schools earning over 15 million in revenue. This list is topped out by Montana State. The Bobcats were ranked 9th in the final FCS poll. It’s not really that much of a surprise that those that put a lot of money into a football program are going to reap the benefits of success. 9 of the 17 schools were ranked inside the FCS top 25 poll at some point in 2023.

Then, I ran a different data visualization for a separate advanced metric to determine how truly good a team is, in relation to revenue. TRACR is a metric that considers how good a team is relative to the strength of its opponent. TRACR accumulates with an offensive and defensive rating, and the difference between the two numbers gives the team an overall rating.
